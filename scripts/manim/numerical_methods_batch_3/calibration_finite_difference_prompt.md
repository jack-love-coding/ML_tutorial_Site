## Stage 1 — ConceptAnalyzer

The root teaching question is: when an implementation exposes function values but its derivative is absent or under suspicion, how can a learner approximate local slope, select a finite-difference step, and interpret failure at both extremes of the step-size sweep? The video must connect four representations without changing symbols: the fixed logits and target, the scalar residual curve `F(b)`, the geometric secant-versus-tangent picture, and the executed numerical error table. The conceptual progression is “construct a residual, sample nearby values, compare stencils, scan h, explain the U-shaped error trend, align with an analytic comparison, then hand the exact same F(b) to root finding.”

Learners must leave with these distinctions. A forward difference uses `F(b+h)` and `F(b)` and has first-order truncation in this context. A central difference uses `F(b+h)` and `F(b-h)` and has second-order truncation, at the cost of evaluating both sides. Smaller h initially makes the secant more local, but once function-value differences approach floating-point resolution, cancellation and division by h amplify numeric noise. The “best sampled h” is conditional on this function, scale, precision, stencil, and sample grid. It is not a universal hyperparameter. An analytic derivative is an independent comparison available in this teaching function, not a proof that every automatic-differentiation implementation is correct.

## Stage 3 — MathematicalEnricher

The mathematical values are locked. At b=0, the mean probability is `0.5015191618696743`. At b=0.35, `F(b)=-0.06078698810485639`. The analytic derivative is

`F'(b)=mean(p_i(b)(1-p_i(b)))`,

and at the probe it is `0.1630982543997438`. The video may round display values, but its source must retain exact anchor strings used by validation. Scan h over `10^-1` through `10^-12`. The best sampled forward step is `10^-7`, approximation `0.16309825379146048`, absolute error `6.082833126086484e-10`. The best sampled central step is `10^-5`, approximation `0.16309825440208314`, absolute error `2.3393509351876673e-12`. At `h=10^-12`, the central error rises to `6.200323353955373e-05`. The forward and central error lines may use rounded arrays from the output but must preserve the order of magnitude and the location of these minima.

Explain truncation geometrically. A wide secant spans visible curvature, so it differs from the tangent even with exact arithmetic. Explain cancellation numerically. At tiny h, `F(b+h)` and `F(b-h)` share most leading digits. Their subtraction discards common leading information, and division by `2h` magnifies the remaining roundoff. Avoid presenting the usual asymptotic error formulas as exact equalities for all h. The animation may say the right side of the log-error curve is dominated by Taylor truncation and the left side by floating-point effects, with a stable region between them.

The function-evaluation language must be precise. Relative to a cached `F(b)`, a forward difference needs one additional call; central difference needs two surrounding calls. The source and page use the label “extra calls.” Do not compare methods only by formula order. Mention that central difference can be more accurate for a moderate h yet has its own floating-point limit. The exact best points occur on the fixed sampled grid, so always say “best sampled,” never “mathematically optimal.”
